Gibson Assembly: Homology-Arm Primer Design for Seamless Multi-Part Cloning

Abstract: The Gibson Assembly module designs primers for homology-based, seamless DNA assembly. It generates 5′ homology arms to bridge adjacent fragments and the linearized vector, optimizes overlap regions towards a target melting temperature (Tm), and provides QC metrics to reduce primer-dimer and hairpin risks in multi-part assemblies.

Introduction

Gibson Assembly enables scarless assembly of multiple DNA fragments in a single reaction through 5′ exonuclease resection, annealing of homologous overlaps, polymerase fill-in, and ligation. The success of the reaction depends strongly on the design of overlap homology arms and robust PCR primer cores for fragment amplification.

Inputs

  • Vector sequence: Circular plasmid sequence and vector linearization strategy (PCR primers or cut positions, depending on workflow).
  • Fragment sequences: One or more inserts/fragments to be assembled in order.
  • Overlap constraints: Target overlap length and/or overlap Tm.
  • Primer constraints: Target core Tm, primer concentration, and salt settings for thermodynamic evaluation.

Design Strategy

Homology arms at each junction

For each fragment junction, the module defines an overlap region shared by the two adjacent fragments in the final construct. It then builds primers by appending these overlap arms to fragment-specific cores.

Overlap optimization

Overlaps can be adjusted in length to approach a target Tm, enabling consistent annealing behavior across multi-part assemblies. The module reports overlap Tm and GC% per junction to allow quick review.

Core design and QC

The 3′ core region is designed to meet a target Tm while maintaining practical length constraints. Cores and full primers are evaluated for dimer/hairpin propensity to reduce failure modes during fragment amplification and assembly PCR steps.

Outputs

  • Primer sets: Vector and per-fragment primers (F/R) with homology arms and binding cores.
  • Junction table: Overlap sequences per junction with predicted Tm/GC.
  • QC report: Hairpin/dimer metrics to identify primers that may require redesign.

Limitations and Practical Notes

  • Very long overlaps or extreme GC content may complicate amplification; users may need to adjust overlap targets or use specialized polymerases.
  • Highly repetitive sequences can yield multiple plausible overlaps; users should verify intended junction uniqueness.
  • Assembly yield also depends on fragment quality, molar ratios, and enzyme mix composition; these are outside primer design scope.
