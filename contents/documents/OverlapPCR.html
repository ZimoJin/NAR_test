Overlap PCR (SOE-PCR): Primer Design for Splicing by Overlap Extension

Abstract: The Overlap PCR (SOE-PCR) module designs primer sets to stitch two or more DNA fragments by overlap extension. It generates outer flanking primers and inner bridge primers whose 5′ tails create defined overlaps between adjacent fragments. The module reports overlap sequences, primer thermodynamics, and QC metrics to support reliable fragment fusion.

Introduction

Splicing by Overlap Extension PCR (SOE-PCR) is a versatile method for assembling fragments without enzymes or ligation by exploiting complementary overlaps generated by primers. It is commonly used to fuse coding sequences, insert tags/linkers, or generate chimeras when only PCR is available.

Inputs

  • Fragment sequences: Two or more DNA fragments (FASTA or plain sequence) in the intended order.
  • Overlap constraints: Target overlap length and/or target overlap Tm.
  • Primer constraints: Target primer Tm, primer concentration, and salt conditions for Tm/QC calculations.

Design Strategy

Bridge primers

For each junction, the module designs a pair of inner primers whose 5′ tails are complementary. The tail sequence constitutes the overlap, while the 3′ region (“core”) binds the template fragment. Overlaps are selected to meet user-defined size/Tm preferences and to avoid excessive self-complementarity.

Flanking primers

Outer primers amplify the final stitched product after the overlap-extension step. Their cores are designed at the ends of the first and last fragments, with Tm balancing against the inner primer cores.

QC integration

All primers are evaluated using the same thermodynamic and secondary-structure routines used across the web server. Reported metrics include Tm, GC%, and 3′-weighted dimer/hairpin propensity.

Outputs

  • Primer list: Outer flanking primers and inner bridge primers per junction.
  • Junction table: Overlap sequence per junction and its predicted Tm/GC.
  • QC report: Hairpin/self-dimer/cross-dimer summaries to flag risky primers before ordering.

Limitations and Practical Notes

  • Highly repetitive or low-complexity regions can produce multiple plausible overlaps; users should validate junction uniqueness in context.
  • Very short fragments may require relaxed core-length constraints to reach target Tm.
  • As with all PCR-based assembly, polymerase choice and extension times should be matched to the final stitched product length.
